Introduction: Why Bridge These Worlds?

We often see a gap between the formal, structured world of mathematics and the dynamic, sometimes messy, ways students actually invent strategies and make sense of numbers. This presentation introduces the Hermeneutic Calculator project â€“ an attempt to bridge:

  • Mathematics Education: Understanding and valuing student's "invented" arithmetic strategies.
  • Formal Mathematics: Using the language of automata (simple computational models) to represent these strategies.
  • Meta-Mathematics & Philosophy: Connecting these models to profound ideas about how systems (mathematical and human) transcend their own limits.

The goal? To show how formalizing student thinking can illuminate not just mathematics, but also connect to fundamental ideas about human freedom and recognition.

More on the Project's Scope and Purpose (click to expand)

This project operates at the intersection of several disciplines, each with its own technical language and traditions. For math educators, the focus is on validating and systematizing the rich variety of student-invented strategies for arithmetic operations.

By formalizing these strategies using automata theory, we can create precise descriptions of how students think about numbers, which helps us better understand student cognition and design more effective instructional approaches.

This approach draws on work by Carpenter et al. (2015) on Cognitively Guided Instruction, which emphasizes the importance of building on children's existing mathematical understanding.

Why Formalize Student Thinking?

The ethical motivation:

  • Many educational approaches treat learners as finite 'things'
  • Such approaches are not just pedagogically limited but mathematically incoherent
  • Formalization provides a mathematical justification for treating learners as capable of unbounded growth

The mathematical insight:

  • Techniques like diagonalization demonstrate how systems transcend their own limits
  • Formal systems can reflect on themselves through their own mechanisms
  • The Hermeneutic Calculator uses these same techniques to demonstrate emergent behaviors in arithmetic

The mathematical concept of diagonalization was pioneered by Georg Cantor (1891) and later extended by Kurt GÃ¶del (1931) in his incompleteness theorems. Haim Gaifman (2006) provides a modern analysis of this technique's significance.

The core of my argument: Treating learners as if they are finite 'things' incapable of growth is fundamentally flawed as a matter of mathematical truth.
More on Diagonalization & Sublation (click to expand)

Diagonalization is a family of techniques where formal systems refer to themselves. In the context of proofs (Euclid, Cantor, GÃ¶del), it leverages a system's own contents and self-reference capacity.

By assuming a totality (a complete list of primes, an enumeration of all reals, a complete formal system), the diagonal argument constructs a new entity that demonstrates the incompleteness of the initial assumption.

Sublation (Hegel's concept) emphasizes overcoming contradictions while preserving their essence. This connection maps from Hegel's arguments about conceptual development to mathematical development itself, unifying theories of mathematical and human development.

Diagonalization in Mathematical Proofs:

  • Euclid's Proof: For any finite list of primes $(A, B, \Gamma)$, we can construct $\delta(A,B,\Gamma) = A \times B \times \Gamma + 1$ that cannot be divided by any of the original primes
  • Cantor's Proof: For any list of real numbers, we can construct a new real number not on the list by systematically flipping digits along the diagonal

More Mathematical Details (click to expand)

Euclid's proof doesn't assert "infinite primes" as commonly claimed. Instead, he shows that for any given list of primes $(A, B, \Gamma)$, we can construct $\delta(A,B,\Gamma) = A \times B \times \Gamma + 1$ that must be either prime itself or measured by a prime not in our original list.

Cantor's 1891 proof uses symbols M and W (not 0s and 1s as often presented), which interestingly are rotationally symmetricâ€”suggesting mathematical truths might appear different but equivalent from different perspectives.

Both proofs follow the same pattern: they take a supposedly complete system, use the system's own elements to construct something new, and thereby show the system cannot contain everything.

Gaifman, H. (2006). Naming and Diagonalization, from Cantor to GÃ¶del to Kleene. Logic Journal of the IGPL, 14(5), 709-728.

Both sublation and diagonalization demonstrate how systems grow and transcend limitations from within - just like the Sneetches had to move beyond their star-based identity.

The Hermeneutic Calculator in Action

The online calculator interface demonstrates how these strategies (modeled as automata) work step-by-step.

Multiplication: Counting by ones visualization

Let's look briefly at Addition: Chunking (Flexible) for 46 + 37:

  1. Start at 46.
  2. Split 37 into 30 (tens) + 7 (ones).
  3. Add the tens chunk: 46 + 30 = 76.
  4. Strategically add ones: Add 4 to make the next ten: 76 + 4 = 80.
  5. Add remaining ones: 3 left (7 - 4 = 3). 80 + 3 = 83.

Implications: Freedom, Recognition, and Math Ed

What does connecting student strategies, automata, and diagonalization offer math educators?

A Richer View of Student Thinking: Instead of just "right" or "wrong," we see student strategies as steps in a developmental process, reflecting their current understanding of number structure (structuration).

Mathematics as Dynamic: Formal proofs of incompleteness (GÃ¶del) and uncountability (Cantor) show that mathematics itself is not a closed, static system. It inherently points beyond its current boundaries.

Connecting Math to Freedom: The pattern of transcendence (diagonalization) seen in math mirrors the human capacity to move beyond fixed definitions or limitations. Recognizing this pattern formally helps argue for educational approaches that respect student agency and potential for growth, rather than confining them to predetermined algorithms.

Recognition: Just as the Sneetches had to recognize each other beyond the stars, understanding mathematics requires recognizing the underlying structures and relationships, not just surface procedures. It involves recognizing the shared human capacity for mathematical reasoning.

These connections between mathematical structure and freedom draw on Robert Brandom's (1994) work on inferentialism and the "space of reasons" in his book Making It Explicit. Harvard University Press.

Philosophical Implications (click to expand)

The connection between mathematical diagonalization and human freedom runs deeper than analogy. In both cases, we see how any attempt to establish final, fixed boundaries leads to their transcendence through the system's own resources.

When we recognize that mathematics itself has this structure, we gain a powerful argument against educational approaches that treat students as mere executors of fixed algorithms. Such approaches contradict the very nature of mathematics.

This perspective resonates with Hegel's concept of mutual recognition (Anerkennung), where genuine freedom emerges through recognizing others as free beings capable of growth and self-determination.

Hegel, G. W. F. (1807/1977). Phenomenology of Spirit (A. V. Miller, Trans.). Oxford University Press.

Brandom, R. (2019). A Spirit of Trust: A Reading of Hegel's Phenomenology. Harvard University Press.

Conclusion & Resources

The Hermeneutic Calculator project argues that by formally modeling student thinking, we uncover deep connections between math education, formal mathematics, and philosophical ideas about growth and transcendence.

This shared pattern (sublation/diagonalization) suggests that learning mathematics is not just about acquiring facts, but about participating in a process of recognizing and moving beyond limits â€“ a process fundamental to both mathematical discovery and human development.

Key Takeaways:

  1. Student "invented" strategies are not just preliminary steps toward standard algorithms, but meaningful mathematical actions in their own right.
  2. The formal structure of mathematics inherently points beyond itself - this is not a flaw but its deepest strength.
  3. Educational approaches that value student agency align with the structure of mathematics itself.
