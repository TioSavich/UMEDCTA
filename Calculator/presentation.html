Introduction: Why Bridge These Worlds?

We often see a gap between the formal, structured world of mathematics and the dynamic, sometimes messy, ways students actually invent strategies and make sense of numbers. This presentation introduces the Hermeneutic Calculator project – an attempt to bridge:

  • Mathematics Education: Understanding and valuing student's "invented" arithmetic strategies.
  • Formal Mathematics: Using the language of automata (simple computational models) to represent these strategies.
  • Meta-Mathematics & Philosophy: Connecting these models to profound ideas about how systems (mathematical and human) transcend their own limits (diagonalization, sublation).

The goal? To show how formalizing student thinking can illuminate not just mathematics, but also connect to fundamental ideas about human freedom and recognition.

Listening to Student Strategies

Children often don't just follow prescribed algorithms. They decompose numbers, rearrange problems, count on, count back, use rounding, and create chunks. Examples include:

  • Adding 46 + 37: Maybe counting on by tens (46 -> 56 -> 66 -> 76), then ones (76 -> 77... -> 83) - (COBO).
  • Subtracting 84 - 29: Maybe rounding 29 to 30, calculating 84 - 30 = 54, then adding 1 back = 55 (Rounding & Adjusting).
  • Multiplying 6 x 7: Maybe thinking (5 x 7) + (1 x 7) = 35 + 7 = 42 (Distributive Reasoning).

These aren't just "errors" or deviations; they are meaningful mathematical actions. The Hermeneutic Calculator project models these using Automata – step-by-step formal descriptions. This allows us to:

  1. Precisely describe the logic within different strategies.
  2. Compare and connect strategies formally.
  3. See deeper structural patterns emerge.

The Core Pattern: Escaping the Box

When we formalize these arithmetic strategies and look at foundational mathematical proofs (like Euclid's proof of infinite primes or Cantor's proof about different sizes of infinity), a recurring pattern emerges. I call this the "Foundational Star" pattern, drawing on philosophy (Hegel) and even children's literature (Dr. Seuss).

Sublation (Hegel / Base Systems)

Think about tally marks: ||||||||| (9). When we add one more, we don't just get ten marks. We negate the individual marks, preserve their value, and transform them into a new unit: 10 (one ten, zero ones).

||||||||| → ||||||||| → 10

The old form is overcome but contained within the new, richer structure.

Diagonalization (Math / Philosophy / Sneetches)

This pattern shows how any attempt to create a complete, bounded system inevitably generates something outside its own boundaries, using the system's own rules.

  • Euclid: Any finite list of primes generates a new prime not on the list.
  • Cantor: Any infinite list of real numbers generates a real number not on the list.
  • Hegel: Trying to grasp pure, immediate "this-here-now" forces us into universal language that transcends the particular.
  • Dr. Seuss's Sneetches: The attempt to define identity by a fixed marker (stars) collapses when the marker becomes interchangeable, forcing recognition beyond the label.

Sneetch Triptych - Visualizing the pattern of transcendence

"The day they decided that Sneetches are Sneetches / And no kind of Sneetch is the best on the beaches. / That day, all the Sneetches forgot about stars / And whether they had one, or not, upon thars."

Both sublation and diagonalization show how systems grow and transcend limitations from within.

Let's look briefly at Addition: Chunking (Flexible) for 46 + 37:

  1. Start at 46.
  2. Split 37 into 30 (tens) + 7 (ones).
  3. Add the tens chunk: 46 + 30 = 76.
  4. Strategically add ones: Add 4 to make the next ten: 76 + 4 = 80.
  5. Add remaining ones: 3 left (7 - 4 = 3). 80 + 3 = 83.

Implications: Freedom, Recognition, and Math Ed

What does connecting student strategies, automata, and diagonalization offer math educators?

  • A Richer View of Student Thinking: Instead of just "right" or "wrong," we see student strategies as steps in a developmental process, reflecting their current understanding of number structure (structuration).
  • Mathematics as Dynamic: Formal proofs of incompleteness (Gödel) and uncountability (Cantor) show that mathematics itself is not a closed, static system. It inherently points beyond its current boundaries.

Division using commutative reasoning - transcending standard algorithms

  • Connecting Math to Freedom: The pattern of transcendence (diagonalization) seen in math mirrors the human capacity to move beyond fixed definitions or limitations. Recognizing this pattern formally helps argue for educational approaches that respect student agency and potential for growth, rather than confining them to predetermined algorithms.
  • Recognition: Just as the Sneetches had to recognize each other beyond the stars, understanding mathematics requires recognizing the underlying structures and relationships, not just surface procedures. It involves recognizing the shared human capacity for mathematical reasoning.

Conclusion & Resources

The Hermeneutic Calculator project argues that by formally modeling student thinking, we uncover deep connections between math education, formal mathematics, and philosophical ideas about growth and transcendence.

This shared pattern (sublation/diagonalization) suggests that learning mathematics is not just about acquiring facts, but about participating in a process of recognizing and moving beyond limits – a process fundamental to both mathematical discovery and human development.

Key Takeaways:

  1. Student "invented" strategies are not just preliminary steps toward standard algorithms, but meaningful mathematical actions in their own right.
  2. The formal structure of mathematics inherently points beyond itself - this is not a flaw but its deepest strength.
  3. Educational approaches that value student agency align with the structure of mathematics itself.

Next Steps: Extending the Hermeneutic Calculator

Where does this research go from here? Two promising directions:

1. Incompatibility Semantics

Integrating Robert Brandom's philosophical framework to analyze the commitments and entitlements involved in mathematical reasoning:

  • How different arithmetic strategies create different patterns of inference
  • When strategies are compatible or incompatible with each other
  • How mathematical reasoning involves normative judgments, not just computations

This approach helps us understand arithmetic as a space of reasons, not just procedures.

2. Emergent Mathematical Behavior

Exploring how automata that model student strategies can demonstrate emergent behavior through self-reference:

  • Building networks of multiple interconnected strategies
  • Using Gödelian encoding to allow automata to "reflect" on their own rules
  • Demonstrating how new strategies emerge from existing ones

This suggests students should be treated as having non-finite potential - just as these machines transcend their initial programming.

"Any system that claims completeness contains within itself the seeds of its own transcendence... No fixed set of strategies is ever complete."

These extensions further reinforce our core argument: the mathematical mind is fundamentally creative and transcendent, not merely computational.
